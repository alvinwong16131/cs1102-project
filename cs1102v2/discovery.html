What We Found

Our Discoveries

Click each discovery to read the full finding

Going into this project, we expected AI to be useful for the general public who have little to no legal knowledge, but not reliable enough for legal professionals who can already provide detailed answers and handle follow-up or exceptional cases that AI would likely miss. In other words, we saw tools like Grok and Gemini as something closer to a smarter web search, a starting point for someone who wants a basic understanding of a legal topic rather than a professional legal resource. What we actually found across all three of our experiments, testing case summarization, document drafting, and legal information retrieval, was both more nuanced and more surprising than that.

From Task 1 — Case Summarisation

Discovery 1 · Task 1
Grok Focuses on Depth While Gemini Focuses on Efficiency
+

Grok is providing more key facts from the case, including facts from the original case. It assumes the users do not know the full background of the case and want to provide as much information as it can. It is an in-depth analysis, and people may find some small evidence there. While Gemini provides an efficient way for users, it only includes the information that is important and writing the facts does not affect the case judgement. Sometimes, Gemini may cause users to miss key facts that can show the plaintiff's or defendant's intention.

Discovery 2 · Task 1
Gemini Is Easier to Read Than Grok
+

Gemini uses bold keywords to help the reader to understand the paragraph's focus, while Grok still uses traditional header-and-bullet-point structure and does not provide the main focus in each paragraph. For example, Gemini can show each legal issue with bullet points and bold keywords, and Grok only writes a paragraph about the legal issue without any bold keywords.

From Task 2 — Document Drafting

Discovery 3 · Task 2
Significant Missing Condition on Both Agreements
+

Both agreements omit a clause on repair and maintenance obligations. In Hong Kong, the law doesn't guarantee that a flat is "fit for habitation" unless it's rented out with furniture. This means if you're renting an empty unit, the landlord isn't legally forced to keep it in a liveable state unless the contract specifically says so. For example, the agreements should specify that the landlord is responsible for structural elements like drains, pipes, and electrical wiring, while the tenant handles internal maintenance and replacing light bulbs.

Discovery 4 · Task 2
Lack of a Detailed Inventory or Schedule of Condition
+

A detailed inventory or schedule of condition was either left blank in Grok's version or completely absent from Gemini's version. Grok mentioned an inventory as "Schedule 1" but left it empty, while Gemini did not refer to it at all. This is a major oversight, especially given that an agreed schedule of condition is often the primary evidence used to resolve deposit disputes at the Lands Tribunal. Without a signed and dated inventory that records the condition of the furniture, appliances, and walls at the start of the tenancy, it becomes very difficult for either party to prove whether damage was caused by the tenant or already existed. As a result, disputes over deposit deductions, which can involve thousands of dollars, are much harder to resolve fairly.

From Task 3 — Legal Information Retrieval

Discovery 5 · Task 3
Accuracy Does Not Equal Usefulness
+

Our first discovery from Task 3 was that being factually correct and being genuinely useful are two very different things. Both Grok and Gemini answered all three questions accurately, but their responses were filled with legal terminology such as "cause of action", "periodic tenancy" and references to specific ordinances like "Cap. 347" without explaining what these terms mean. For someone with no legal background, this creates a dangerous situation. The confident and organised tone of the answer makes it sound completely trustworthy, which means most people in an urgent situation would accept it and act on it without questioning whether they actually understood it. The convincing presentation of the answer becomes the problem itself, because it removes the instinct to verify.

Discovery 6 · Task 3
Regional Accuracy Is Trained, Not Prompted
+

Our second discovery was that two AIs given the exact same Hong Kong specific prompt can produce very different levels of regional accuracy, and the reason appears to come down to how each model retrieves information. Grok behaved more like a live search engine, pulling results from multiple internet sources during our conversation, which meant its answers were a combination of Hong Kong specific and general international legal information, making them broader but less locally precise. Gemini on the other hand appeared to reason from its training data which seemed to contain stronger Hong Kong legal knowledge, producing answers that cited specific local ordinances and amendments more consistently. This surprised us because we assumed that specifying the region in our prompt would level the playing field between both models, but regional accuracy turns out to be determined by what the model was trained on, not just what you ask.

Discovery 7 · Task 3
How AI Retrieves Information Changes Its Legal Accuracy
+

Our third discovery was about how each AI retrieves information, and how that affects legal accuracy. Grok is built to pull from live internet sources by default, which is why our screenshots showed it citing over 45 web sources per answer. Gemini on the other hand uses a thinking mode that reasons from its training data first before searching, which we could see from the "Show thinking" indicator in our screenshots. This difference in how they retrieve information turned out to matter significantly for legal questions. Gemini's deeper training on structured legal content gave it stronger regional knowledge, while Grok's live web retrieval made its answers broader but less locally precise. This was something we could not have predicted just from knowing which AI we were using.

Discovery 8 · Task 3
Usage Limits Are a Reliability Risk in Urgent Situations
+

Our fourth discovery came from an unexpected practical limitation. During our experiment, Grok reached its free usage limit after just two questions, forcing us to start a completely new session. This meant the entire conversation history was lost and could not be transferred to the new chat. While a two hour cooldown period might be manageable in casual use, in an urgent legal situation where someone needs to ask multiple connected questions in one sitting, this is a serious reliability concern. What made this more interesting is what happens next for a user in this situation. Switching to a different AI model sounds like a simple solution, but as our own experiments showed, the same legal question asked to different AI models can produce different answers with different levels of accuracy. Someone who has only ever used Grok may not be familiar with how Gemini presents information, and may not know which answer to trust. This means the choice of AI model is not just a preference but something that can directly affect the quality of legal information a person receives, which is a risk most users would never think to consider.

Discovery 9 · Task 3
The People Who Need Legal AI Most Are Least Able to Use It Effectively
+

Our fifth discovery was about how much prompt quality affects the usefulness of AI legal answers, and why this is a bigger problem than it first appears. In our experiment, when we asked both AIs for a specific number with a clearly structured question, we received much clearer and more useful answers than when we asked more open questions. This means the person asking the question needs to already understand the topic well enough to ask it precisely, which creates a fundamental contradiction. The people who need legal AI the most are the general public who have little legal knowledge, but they are also the least equipped to write a prompt specific enough to get a useful answer. A lawyer or legal professional would know exactly what to ask, but they already have the knowledge to begin with. For the general public, asking a legal question to an AI is nothing like consulting a real lawyer. In a real legal consultation, the lawyer helps the client figure out what they even need to know, guiding the conversation and asking follow up questions to understand the situation fully. With AI, the entire burden of knowing what to ask falls on the user from the very first prompt, and without that knowledge, they will likely receive a broad answer where only half the information is relevant to their actual situation. This gap between who needs legal AI and who can actually use it effectively is something that neither Grok nor Gemini addresses.

Overall Conclusion

Across all three experiments, a consistent picture emerged. Grok and Gemini are both capable tools that can handle legal tasks with reasonable accuracy, but accuracy alone does not make them reliable for real world legal use. Both models struggled with the same fundamental problem: they produce answers that sound authoritative and complete, but require the user to already have enough legal knowledge to evaluate what they are reading. For the general public, this creates a false sense of security. For legal professionals, they offer genuine value as a fast reference tool, but only when the professional can independently verify what the AI produces. The most honest conclusion we can draw from this project is that AI is not yet a substitute for legal advice. It is a starting point, and like any starting point, knowing where it ends is just as important as knowing where it begins.
