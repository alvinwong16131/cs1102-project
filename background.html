Background

What are Large Language Models, and how are they being used in law?

What Are Large Language Models?

Large Language Models (LLMs) are a type of artificial intelligence that works like a conversational assistant. You ask a question and it gives you a direct, organised answer, similar to a search engine but instead of showing you a list of links, it processes the information and responds in natural language as if you were talking to a person. At first glance this seems like a more reliable and efficient way to find information, but as we discovered through our experiments, accuracy is not always guaranteed.

How LLMs Are Trained

LLMs learn through a process called Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback. Fine tuning comes first, where human trainers demonstrate examples of good question and answer pairs to the model, then rank the model's own outputs from best to worst. A reward system uses these rankings to gradually push the model towards giving answers that match human preferences. On top of this, LLMs are trained on massive amounts of text data from the internet, books and other sources, which allows them to match patterns in that training data to whatever question a user asks and produce a response in natural, readable language.

LLMs in the Legal Industry

In the legal industry, LLMs are increasingly being used to assist with tasks that are time consuming and tedious, such as legal research, document drafting, proofreading and case summarization. Much like looking up a known word in a dictionary to verify its exact definition, lawyers often use AI to quickly confirm information they already have a general understanding of, rather than reading through hundreds of pages of case law or legislation manually. Real world tools built on LLMs such as Harvey AI, which is used by major law firms, and Westlaw AI, which assists with legal research and case retrieval, have already been adopted in the legal industry to speed up these workflows.

Examples of legal AI in practice: Harvey AI is used by major international law firms to automate document review and legal drafting. Westlaw AI integrates AI-powered research into one of the world's largest legal databases, helping lawyers find relevant case law and statutes in seconds rather than hours.

The Risk of Misplaced Confidence

But this convenience comes with a real danger, particularly in high stakes fields like law. When searching the internet, users naturally encounter multiple sources and links which forces them to read carefully and apply critical thinking before reaching a conclusion. With AI, this instinct is bypassed. The answer arrives in a single, organised, confident response written in natural human language, making it far more convincing than it may deserve to be.

Most people would not think to cross check an AI answer with a second AI model or an official source, and since LLMs generate answers by matching patterns in training data rather than recalling verified facts the way a documented source would, there is a real risk of receiving false information presented so convincingly that the user has no reason to question it.
